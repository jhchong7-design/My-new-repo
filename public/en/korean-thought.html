📜 Korean Bible Reception History (1600–1900)

The history of how the Bible was received in Korea is remarkably unique. Unlike many Western nations where missionaries brought the Bible and disseminated it, in Korea, the people themselves obtained and read the Scriptures, organized faith communities, and even performed baptisms before any missionaries arrived on Korean soil.

Beginning in the 1600s, Western learning texts (Seohakseo, 西學書) and Catholic catechisms entering the Joseon Dynasty made the Bible known in a substantive way. The Confucian scholars of Joseon took special note of what Professor Jung calls the "Chinese Scripture Period" (漢文聖經期) — a period when biblical content was transmitted through Classical Chinese texts. Remarkably, by the 1790s, even while Joseon was marginalizing the Korean alphabet (Hangul), 30.68% of the Four Gospels had already been translated into Hangul.

"It was not missionaries who brought the Bible to Korea. Before any missionary set foot on Korean soil, Koreans had already obtained and were reading the Bible on their own. Both Protestantism and Catholicism in Korea share the extraordinary history of communities being organized and baptisms being performed before the arrival of missionaries." — Professor Jung Joong-Ho, 300 Years of the Korean Bible

Tracing back even further, Catholic catechisms were discovered in Korea from the time of the Japanese invasions (Imjin War). Giwargis (闊里吉思), a Mongol Empire official dispatched to Goryeo (Korea), was a Christian who, inspired by Christian principles, worked to reform the slave system of the Goryeo Dynasty. These findings reveal that the roots of Korean Christianity run far deeper and wider than is commonly known.

📖 Korean Biblical Interpretation

Korean biblical interpretation is an academic endeavor that seeks to read the Bible's message within Korea's unique historical and cultural context. Professor Jung has conducted groundbreaking research connecting the Old Testament concept of Jubilee (禧年) with Korea's own history of slave liberation and debt cancellation.

Research into the 1800s distinguished between Catholic and Protestant contributions: it is noteworthy that the Catholic Church translated as many as eight Psalms into Hangul. In Manchuria, missionary John Ross (1882) and in Japan, Yi Su-jeong (1885) translated the New Testament into Korean. Professor Jung has compared the strengths and weaknesses of these translations with later versions, and significantly, has demonstrated that Catholic translations influenced subsequent Protestant translations.

By carefully tracking the reception of Scripture — distinguishing between the New Testament, Apocrypha, and Old Testament, and further subdividing the OT into Pentateuch, Prophets, and Writings — Professor Jung has systematically documented the independent tradition of Korean biblical interpretation.

🎎 Korean Culture & the Bible

The encounter between Korea's traditional thought systems — Confucianism, Buddhism, and Shamanism — and the Bible constitutes not merely a collision but a rich history of profound dialogue. Professor Jung's research examines how biblical principles resonate with Korean cultural values and what unique interpretations have emerged from this meeting.

In particular, his work analyzes how Korean concepts of community, filial piety (孝), and justice connect with the social ethics and legal spirit of the Old Testament, exploring the roots of a uniquely Korean Christian theology through these connections.

Korean Christianity possesses a tradition of "voluntary reception" that is unique in the global history of Christianity. This distinctive historical experience is the wellspring of both the originality and creativity of Korean biblical interpretation.
