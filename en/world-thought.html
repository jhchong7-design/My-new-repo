🏛️ The Ancient Near East & the Bible

The Old Testament was formed within the historical and cultural context of the Ancient Near East. The legal codes, literature, and religious traditions of civilizations such as Mesopotamia, Egypt, and Canaan play a crucial role in understanding the background of the Bible.

Professor Jung studies the history and religion of Israel within this Ancient Near Eastern context, exploring how Israel's distinctive Yahweh faith interacted with surrounding cultures while maintaining its unique identity. His landmark paper "Were There Yahwistic Sanctuaries in Babylon?" (published in the Asia Journal of Theology) academically investigates the reality of Israelite religion during the Babylonian exile period, asking whether formal worship of Yahweh took place within Babylon itself.

The creation narratives, flood stories, and law codes of the Ancient Near East show remarkable similarities with the Old Testament. Yet understanding how Israel's distinctive theological message differentiates itself from these parallels lies at the heart of comparative research.

🌐 Modern Thought & the Bible

The modern world, shaped by scientific advancement, secularization, and pluralism, faces the ongoing challenge of reinterpreting the meaning of Scripture. Professor Jung's book Dialogue between Scripture and Science explores the tension and harmony between faith and reason, between the Bible and modern scientific discoveries.

Furthermore, through A Meaningful Life: Modern Society and Christianity, he presents biblical responses to the existential questions facing modern individuals, demonstrating that the Bible — an ancient text — still delivers powerfully relevant messages for today.

As Director of the Asian Christianity Research Institute, Professor Jung has also actively conducted comparative studies on how Christianity and the Bible have been received and interpreted within the diverse cultural and religious traditions across Asia.

🔍 Comparative Studies

Comparative study of world thought and the Bible pursues a broad perspective spanning East and West, ancient and modern. Professor Jung's research encompasses the following areas:

⚖️

Ancient Law Codes & Mosaic Law

Comparative study of Ancient Near Eastern law codes (such as the Code of Hammurabi) with Old Testament law, illuminating the distinctiveness of Israelite legislation.

🏺

Israelite Prophetic Tradition

Analysis of the Israelite prophetic tradition in comparison with Ancient Near Eastern divination practices, examining the social function of prophecy.

🌏

Asian Christianity Compared

Comparative research on the history of Bible reception and interpretation across Korea, China, Japan, Southeast Asia, and other Asian regions.

🔬

Science & Faith Dialogue

Exploration of how the discoveries of modern science and the worldview of the Bible can engage in meaningful dialogue.

The concept of "Glocal" combines "Global" and "Local." The Bible carries a universal (Global) message, yet it is uniquely interpreted and received within the culture and history of each locality (Local). Bible Glocal aims to study precisely this meeting point of the two dimensions.
